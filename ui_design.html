UI Design

Design Principles AI SLOP gotta make it moree specific to widget

The widget should always keep users informed about what is going on through appropriate feedback within a reasonable time.

Purpose: Users should never feel lost or confused on where they are going, additionally they shouldn't be misled by outdated information.

Implementation:

  • Display loading indicators during data processing.
  • Show success or error messages after actions (e.g., saving a location).
  • Use progress bars or step indicators for multi-step processes.
  • Show warnings for hazardous locations or outdated accessibility features that may not be there.

The widget should be in multiple languages, with vocabulary familiar to the user.

Purpose: Reduces the learning curve and makes the app more intuitive.

Implementation:

  • Use familiar terms like "Saved Locations" instead of technical jargon.
  • Use icons and labels that are universally understood (e.g., a house icon for "Home").
  • Align workflows with real-world tasks (e.g., saving a location mimics saving a bookmark).

Users often perform actions by mistake and need a clearly marked "emergency exit" to leave the unwanted state without having to go through an extended process.

Purpose: Gives users confidence to explore the app without fear of making irreversible mistakes.

Implementation:

  • Include "Back" or "Cancel" buttons prominently.
  • Allow users to undo actions (e.g., unsave a location).
  • Provide clear exit points from workflows (e.g., closing a modal or returning to the home screen).

Users should not have to wonder whether different words, situations, or actions mean the same thing. Follow platform conventions and maintain consistency.

Purpose: Reduces confusion and improves learnability.

Implementation:

  • Use consistent button styles, fonts, and colors across all pages.
  • Follow platform-specific design guidelines (e.g., Material Design for Android).
  • Ensure terminology is consistent (e.g., "Profile" is always called "Profile").

Even better than good error messages is a careful design that prevents a problem from occurring in the first place.

Purpose: Minimizes user frustration and prevents mistakes.

Implementation:

  • Use confirmation dialogs for critical actions (e.g., deleting a saved location).
  • Validate inputs in real-time (e.g., checking for valid email formats during sign-up).
  • Provide clear instructions and constraints (e.g., character limits for text fields).

Minimize the user's memory load by making objects, actions, and options visible. The user should not have to remember information from one part of the dialogue to another.

Purpose: Reduces cognitive load and makes the app easier to use.

Implementation:

  • Display saved locations prominently on the home screen.
  • Use visual cues like icons and labels to remind users of available actions.
  • Provide autocomplete or suggestions for search fields.

Accelerators — unseen by the novice user — may often speed up the interaction for the expert user, allowing the system to cater to both inexperienced and experienced users.

Purpose: Enhances usability for both new and experienced users.

Implementation:

  • Provide shortcuts for frequent actions (e.g., quick access to saved locations).
  • Allow customization of the interface (e.g., rearranging widgets).
  • Support keyboard shortcuts for desktop users.

Dialogues should not contain information that is irrelevant or rarely needed. Every extra unit of information competes with the relevant units of information and diminishes their relative visibility.

Purpose: Keeps the interface clean and focused on the task at hand.

Implementation:

  • Avoid cluttering the interface with unnecessary elements.
  • Use whitespace effectively to separate sections.
  • Prioritize essential information and hide advanced options behind menus.

These are the sketches of the planned interface for the mobile application. The ideas behind these sketches are derived from the key requirements that were identified from the stakeholders earlier, as well as the personas that were crafted to identify the target user base of the app. The sketches are designed to be simple and intuitive, with a focus on accessibility and ease of use.

A page that displays all the locations that the user has saved with the unique three word identifications

User's profile page with personal information and a list of reviews they have made on different accessible locations with information on how (and if) those reviews have been addressed

The home page, consisting of the map of nearby area and the user's current location. The user can select different data layers (outdoor, indoor or warnings) and filter the icons on the map as well as search for specific premises

Improved Sketches

Upon gathering feedback, we decided to proceed with this set of sketches (sketches 2). The users informed us that this version has much more intuitive UI and provides an overall better user experience which is more attuned of people with accessibility needs.

Prototype

Our product is designed to be used by people with disabilities, so it is important that the content is dynamic and available on all platofrms, and can be used on a webpage or a mobile application, or even desktop. Exploring the design of our product in the sketches and evaluation, a widget that companies can embed in their webpage is more accessible than mobile application, as psychological overhead is reduced.

Additionally QR Codes outside organisations using our widget can be used to launch applications rapidly.

We used Figma to build a prototype to explore our sketch in greater depth and evaluate its user experience and practicality. The pictures are the mobile web page with necessary features and specifications. The blue arrows show the navigation of pages when you use the widgets of the web page.

Feedback

Feedback from the users was positive, with the majority of users finding the interface intuitive and easy to use. The users appreciated the simplicity of the design and the straightforward navigation. The users also found the interface to be accessible and inclusive, with features that cater to a wide range of users, including those with disabilities. The users also appreciated the visual design of the interface, with many commenting on the clean and modern look of the app. Overall, the feedback from the users was very positive, with many users expressing a desire to use the app in the future.

Final Design

After gathering feedback from the users and evaluating the prototypes, we have come up with the final design for the mobile application. The final design incorporates the feedback from the users and the improvements made to the prototypes.
